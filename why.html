About The Village

We're the team building Kith—the app that helps you coordinate childcare with 10-15 trusted people, the way humans have always raised children

We started building Kith because we were exhausted.

Not from our kids—from the constant mental load of coordinating care. The guilt of asking the same neighbor again. The WhatsApp group chaos. The impossible choice between "call in sick to work" or "bring your kid to the presentation."

The problem isn't that people don't want to help each other. It's that we're trying to run a complex logistics operation using tools designed for casual chatting.

Why "Kith"?

Humans can maintain about 150 meaningful relationships (Dunbar's number). But research on our closest relationships shows we devote two-thirds of our social time to just 15 people: an inner core of about 5 people, plus 10 more in our close circle.

For 99% of human history, children were raised by 8-14 active caregivers daily. Hunter-gatherer societies show caregiver-to-child ratios exceeding 5:1. Multiple adults—grandparents, aunts, uncles, neighbors, older siblings—all pitched in. This wasn't charity; it was how survival worked.

Kith helps you build that 10-15 person support network and coordinate with them effectively—without the cognitive load of remembering who owes what, who's good at what, and who to ask for help.

What the research taught us

For 99% of human history, children were raised by 8-14 active caregivers daily. Multiple adults—grandparents, aunts, uncles, neighbors, older siblings—all pitched in. This wasn't charity; it was survival. A human child requires an estimated 10-13 million calories to grow to independence. That workload is literally too great for 1-2 people.

Then came nuclear families. A historical blip. Just decades or centuries old depending on where you live. We scattered the village, moved for jobs, isolated ourselves. And now 1-2 people are expected to do what 8-14 used to share. (Read more about alloparenting)

The exhaustion modern parents feel isn't personal failure. It's a rational response to being asked to do something humans were never designed to do alone.

Our Great Hope

We're not interested in the nuclear family debate. That's a distraction.

We're cultivating the regenerative family movement—families that look beyond isolation and rebuild cooperative networks. Community alloparenting as the emerging paradigm. Not instead of parents, but alongside them.

Our hope is simple: What if we could give parents the infrastructure to rebuild what we lost? Not nostalgia for some idealized past, but practical tools to coordinate the 10-15 people who want to help but don't know how.

What we believe

Community over isolation

Humans evolved to raise children in groups. Nuclear families doing it alone is a historical anomaly that's breaking us.

Infrastructure over goodwill

Reciprocity needs systems, not guilt. Time tracking should be automatic. Asking for help should be frictionless.

Diverse reciprocity, not tit-for-tat

You watched someone's kids? They don't owe you childcare back. Maybe they help with your resume. Maybe they teach your kid guitar. Skills are currency—everyone has something valuable to offer.

Trust over algorithms

Your friend's judgment about their cousin is worth more than a stranger's 5-star rating. Networks grow through vouching, not marketing.

Free coordination, paid options

Time banking should always be free. But parents also need the option to pay network members directly or hire vetted professionals when their circle can't cover it.

Why "Kith"? Why "The Village"?

"Kith" is an old English word meaning "friends and neighbors"—people you know who aren't family (kin). Your kith are your people, your community, the ones who show up. It's the perfect word for what we're building: networks of trusted people beyond blood relations who help raise children together.

"It takes a village to raise a child" isn't just a nice saying—it's how humans have always done this. Extended family, neighbors, community members all contributing to childcare. But modern life scattered both kin and kith. We moved for jobs. Family lives across the country. We don't know our neighbors' names.

We're living in an accelerated timeline with too many people to keep up with. Anthropologist Robin Dunbar estimated we can maintain about 150 meaningful relationships, but we devote most of our time and energy to just 10-15 people. That's your core circle. Your kith.

The Village is our company name because we're building infrastructure to rebuild what we lost. Kith is our app name because that's what you're building—your circle of 10-15 trusted people (friends, neighbors, community) who actively help each other raise kids.

Not a replacement for real relationships. Digital infrastructure to organize them. To make coordination actually work instead of relying on guilt, memory, and whoever happens to answer their phone.

We're building this because we need it

The Village team are parents, caregivers, and people who've watched friends burn out trying to coordinate care through WhatsApp. We're building Kith—the tool we wish we had when childcare fell through at 7am. When we needed a break but felt too guilty to ask. When we wanted to help but didn't know who needed it.

Kith is infrastructure for the 10-15 people you're already trying to coordinate with. We handle the logistics so you can focus on the relationships.

The 2AM Text I'll Never Forget

January 2026

2:17am. My phone buzzes. It's Sarah, texting our neighborhood group: "Emergency. Husband in ambulance. Kids (3 & 5) asleep upstairs. Anyone awake?"

I threw on clothes and drove over. By 2:35am I was sitting in her living room while she followed the ambulance to the ER. Her kids never woke up. I made coffee, answered work emails, and was there when they got up at 6:30am asking where mommy was.

Sarah's husband was fine—kidney stone, not heart attack. But here's what haunts me: What if nobody had been awake? What if we didn't have that group chat? What if she'd hesitated to ask because it felt like "too much"?

For 200,000 years, humans lived in groups where someone was always awake. Always nearby. Always available. Anthropologists call this "cooperative breeding." We literally evolved expecting help.

Modern parenting is the experiment. Nuclear families isolated in houses, expected to handle every emergency alone. And we're failing—not because parents are weak, but because the setup is insane.

Kith is infrastructure for 2AM emergencies. Not just cute playdates. Real crisis response from people who actually show up.

My Daughter Asked Why We Don't Have a Village

December 2025

"Why don't we live near Grandma?" my 8-year-old asked last week. "In my book, everyone lives together."

She's reading Little House on the Prairie. Grandparents, aunts, uncles, neighbors all within walking distance. Communities that raised children together because there was no other option.

I tried explaining jobs, rent prices, opportunity. She cut me off: "That sounds lonely."

She's right. It is lonely. Studies of hunter-gatherer societies show children historically had 8-14 active daily caregivers. Not because parents were negligent—because that's how human biology works. Multiple adults providing care, teaching, attention, affection.

Now? My daughter has me, her dad, and occasionally grandparents via FaceTime. On a good week, maybe her teacher and a friend's parent. That's it. That's her "village."

We can't move everyone back to 1870. But we can rebuild coordinated care networks with people we trust. Digital infrastructure for the village our kids are wired to expect.

Kith won't give my daughter her grandma next door. But it'll help her have 10-15 adults who know her name, show up for her, and model what community actually looks like.

The Childcare Co-op That Almost Died

November 2025

I interviewed a childcare co-op in Portland last month. 12 families, started three years ago, beautiful vision. Shared babysitting, skill exchanges, potlucks. The dream.

They almost disbanded last spring. Why? Nobody could remember who owed what.

"I watched Jamie's kids for 6 hours last month, but did she watch mine? I think so? Or was that Sarah?" One mom kept a spreadsheet. Another used a notebook. A third just… tried to remember. They spent more time arguing about balance than actually helping each other.

The breaking point: One member asked for help during a rough week. Got accused of "taking too much." She quit in tears. Two more families left in solidarity. The co-op nearly collapsed.

Here's the thing: Time banking and mutual aid worked for millennia because communities were small and saw each other daily. Social memory handled the accounting. You can't scale that to modern life where everyone's busy, exhausted, and cognitively overloaded.

The vision was right. The infrastructure was missing.

Kith is the spreadsheet nobody has to maintain. The accounting that happens automatically. The infrastructure that lets the beautiful vision actually work without burning everyone out.

That Portland co-op? They're joining our beta. Still together. Now with tools that match their values.

The Grandmother Hypothesis Changed Everything

October 2025

Most mammals die shortly after they can't reproduce. Humans? We live decades past menopause. Why?

Grandmothers.

The "Grandmother Hypothesis" in evolutionary biology argues that human longevity evolved because grandmothers helped raise grandchildren. Mothers could have more kids, closer together, because grandmothers freed them up. Those genes—for long life, for alloparenting—are still in us.

My mother-in-law lives 800 miles away. She visits twice a year. FaceTimes on Sundays. That's it. My kids are biologically wired to expect her nearby, teaching them things, giving me breaks, being part of daily life. Instead, she's a voice on a screen.

This isn't her fault or mine. It's economics. Jobs. Housing. Mobility. All the things that scattered the village.

But here's what breaks me: My kids will never know what it's like to have a grandmother who just… shows up. Who's there when they're sick, when I'm overwhelmed, when we need an extra pair of hands. They're missing out on millions of years of evolutionary heritage because we decided nuclear families in separate houses made economic sense.

Kith can't bring back grandmothers. But it can help us rebuild networks of "allograndparents"—older adults in the community who want to be involved with kids, who have time and skills, who miss being needed.

The biology is still there. We just need infrastructure to activate it.

Your Great-Grandmother's Notebook

September 2025

I found my great-grandmother's diary from 1947. Rural Kansas. She logged everything: "Helped Ethel with canning (4 hours). Ethel watched kids while I went to doctor (2 hours). Still owe her 2."

She wasn't using an app. She was just… keeping track. Because that's what communities did. You help me, I help you, we remember the balance, we stay roughly even over time.

Time banking isn't some hippie invention. It's what humans did before money became the primary way to transact with strangers. You traded labor with neighbors because you'd see them again next week, next month, next year. Reputation mattered. Reciprocity mattered.

What changed? Scale. Mobility. Cognitive load.

My great-grandmother had maybe 30 people in her community she traded with regularly. She saw them at church, at the store, at school events. Social memory worked because the network was stable and small.

I have 200+ contacts in my phone. I've lived in three cities in ten years. I have 47 unread texts. I cannot hold this in my head.

The principle still works. The infrastructure doesn't.

Kith isn't inventing time banking. We're adapting your great-grandmother's system for the reality of modern life: distributed networks, high cognitive load, nobody keeping notebooks anymore.

Same values. Better infrastructure. That's it.

The Reason Burnout Feels Like Failure

August 2025

A mother posted in a parenting group last week: "I feel like I'm failing. I can't keep up. Everyone else seems fine."

147 replies. Every single one: "Me too." "I'm drowning." "I thought it was just me."

Here's why burnout feels like personal failure: We've been sold the lie that good parents don't need help. That struggling means you're not cut out for this. That asking for support is admitting weakness.

But here's the biological reality: Human children require 10-13 million calories from birth to independence. One or two people cannot generate that energy alone. It's thermodynamically impossible while also working jobs, maintaining homes, and staying mentally stable.

Evolutionary biologist Sarah Blaffer Hrdy spent decades documenting this: humans evolved as cooperative breeders. Mothers who tried solo didn't survive. Their children didn't survive. We exist because our ancestors had help.

The exhaustion you feel isn't character weakness. It's your body screaming that you're trying to do something biologically impossible. You're responding rationally to an irrational setup.

Kith's entire purpose is to make asking for help structurally normal instead of emotionally fraught. To build infrastructure where reciprocity is tracked, burnout is prevented, and nobody has to feel guilty for being human.

You're not failing. The system is failing you. Let's build a better one.

What My Son's Therapist Actually Told Me

July 2025

My 10-year-old has been seeing a therapist for anxiety. Last month she asked to meet with me alone. I braced for bad news.

"Your son is fine," she said. "He's anxious because you're anxious. And you're anxious because you're trying to do something impossible."

She pulled up research. Children in hunter-gatherer societies have 5+ active caregivers minimum. Multiple adults providing security, attention, teaching, affection. Kids aren't stressed because the load is distributed. No single adult is overwhelmed.

"Your son sees you stretched thin," she continued. "He worries about you. Kids are wired to expect multiple stable adults. When there's only one or two, and those adults are obviously struggling, children get anxious. They're responding rationally to an unstable situation."

My son doesn't need therapy. He needs more adults in his life who aren't his exhausted parents.

I cried in her office. Not because my kid was broken—because I wasn't failing him. The setup was failing both of us. His anxiety was a reasonable response to an unreasonable situation.

We're building Kith so other parents don't have to have this conversation. So kids grow up with 10-15 adults who know them, show up for them, and model what stable community looks like.

Childhood anxiety is rising not because kids are weaker—but because we dismantled the infrastructure they're biologically wired to expect.

Let's rebuild it.
